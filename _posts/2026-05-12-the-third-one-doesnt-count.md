I want to be clear about something upfront: the third car doesn't count. It was my own.

---

My career didn't start in a dealership. It didn't start with a title or a plan or any particular vision of where I'd end up. It started, like a lot of things in my life, with a combination of enthusiasm, ambition, and a complete absence of talent for the thing I was enthusiastic about.

In high school I worked the concession stands at Western Michigan University. After graduation — still young, still figuring it out — I took a job as a stockman at a clothing store in downtown Kalamazoo. Not exactly a calling. I was smart enough to know that, and restless enough to do something about it.

So I ran off to Phoenix, Arizona.

The Phoenix Institute of Technology had a program for learning to work on cars, and something about that appealed to me in a way I couldn't quite articulate at the time. I understand it better now: I have a brain that loves systems. Loves understanding how things work, how the pieces connect, what happens when you change one variable. As an ADHD kid in a vocational school, I aced every class. Not because I was trying especially hard — because the material made sense to me in a way that felt almost effortless. I could visualize every nut and bolt in an engine. I understood the theory completely.

What I couldn't do, it turned out, was actually fix a car.

---

I came back to Michigan and went to work as a technician. I lasted less than a year, and I want to tell you honestly what those months felt like, because I think it matters.

I would watch my coworkers — genuinely talented technicians — pull a vehicle into the bay, diagnose it, repair it, pull it out, and pull the next one in. Efficient. Fluid. Like they were having a conversation with the car. Meanwhile, my brain was still working through the diagnosis on the first one. I could *see* the solution. I understood the problem. And I absolutely could not make my hands do the work.

There's a difference between knowledge and talent that nobody really teaches you before you need to learn it. Knowledge is knowing how an engine works. Talent is being able to reach in there and make it right. I had the first one. I was painfully short on the second.

And then there were the fires.

The first car — a customer's vehicle — caught fire while I was working on it. I will not be elaborating on the specific circumstances. The second car also caught fire. I will also not be elaborating on those circumstances. These were not small events. They were the kind of events that cause a young man to sit quietly with himself for a few minutes and have an honest conversation about career trajectory.

*(The third car caught fire too. But that one was my own, so it doesn't count.)*

I decided, with great clarity, that I was considerably better suited to working *with* customers than I was to working on their vehicles. I moved from technician to service writer — and something shifted. Talking to people, understanding what they needed, translating between the customer and the shop — that came naturally in a way that wrenches never had.

---

This was a different era in automotive retail. We wrote work orders on paper. We tracked parts sales on index cards that we'd ship off to Volkswagen, who would process the data and mail back a giant green-bar printout with a suggested monthly parts order. It was slow and manual and nobody thought much about it.

Then one day, the owner of the store walked over and asked me — casually, the way you'd ask someone to grab lunch — *"John? You know how to spell computer, right?"*

I was already a nerd. I said yes cheerfully. And that was it. That was the pivot point. I became the IT Manager, and then moved into parts — because that's where most of the computers were kept at the time — and suddenly I was doing both: managing the technology *and* learning everything I could about parts operations.

The aptitude for systems that had made me a strong student in Phoenix, and a poor technician in the shop, turned out to be exactly what you needed to make a dealership's computer systems sing. Other Volkswagen stores started calling, asking for help writing reports, configuring setups, making things run faster. I ran twin-axial cable through the ceilings. I set up and maintained printers. I learned everything I could absorb from the people around me.

---

Three of them I want to name, because this story isn't just mine.

**Guy "Mac" McGee** was one of the funniest men I've ever met in my life. Full stop. The kind of funny where you couldn't be in a bad mood if he was in the room. Mac is gone now, and I still miss him when I think about those years.

**Dean Binkley** was a high-strung troublemaker in the best possible way — the kind of person who kept things interesting and pushed back on the comfortable answers. Dean is gone too. I think about him more than people might expect.

And then there was **Jeff Fowler**.

Jeff was my boss. My friend. My hero — and I don't use that word casually. Jeff is one of those rare people who genuinely cares about *how* he does *what* he does. Not just the outcome. The integrity of the whole thing. Working for someone like that when you're young and hungry and still figuring out who you want to be — it leaves a mark. A good one. Jeff's still a good friend, and I'm happy to say so publicly.

---

I was young. I was inexperienced. I had managed to set fire to two customer vehicles and one of my own. I had a vocational degree I couldn't fully use and a technology skill set I'd developed out of pure curiosity and an offhand question from an owner who needed someone who could spell *computer*.

Looking back, I can see the puzzle pieces assembling themselves into something I couldn't have designed on purpose. The ADHD brain that couldn't focus a wrench could map a network. The kid who loved talking to customers would spend thirty-five years teaching people how to serve them better. The technician who couldn't fix a car would eventually help a hundred and forty-eight dealerships fix the systems that run their entire operation.

I didn't plan any of it. But I showed up, said yes to the right questions, and paid attention to the right people.

That's still the whole strategy, if I'm being honest.
